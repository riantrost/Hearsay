# Decisions

One paragraph per settled fork. Once an item lands here, treat it as closed — don't re-litigate it in a session unless new information genuinely overturns it. (Convention carried from Litany via Fragments. These pins carry the *decision*; the narrative context lives in [hearsay-vision.md](hearsay-vision.md).)

## The working title is Hearsay
Locked 2026-07-16. The name is the thesis: the record is testimony, not truth — the same battle remembered differently by everyone who was there, and that plurality is what makes the record worth keeping. Candidates *Annals*, *Waypoints*, and *Chronicle* were considered and passed over as describing a record rather than a stance about one.

## Authority is split by layer, never enforced by moderation
The GM (generalized to *campaign owner* in GM-less systems like Frostgrave — the layer model doesn't care whether that person also plays) owns canon: the map, locations, reveals, story arc, and where event pins land. Players own testimony: per-event journals immutable by anyone but their author. The GM curates placement and visibility of events, never the words — canon never requires editing anyone's testimony because testimony is definitionally subjective. Contradiction between testimonies is not an error state; it is the artifact. Merge and canonical summaries are refused: they flatten the plurality the app exists to keep (2026-07-16).

## Testimony is visible between players by default; sealing is a GM setting
Players read each other's testimony freely — plural memory at full strength — unless the GM seals it per-campaign (private until conclusion) or per-entry (for tables that run secrets). Sealing is disclosure control, the same family as fog; it never grants editing (settled 2026-07-16). Open sub-question, deferred to the Frostgrave test: whether competitive play needs a "sealed until both sides have written" mode so rival accounts of the same battle aren't shaped by whoever writes first.

## Fog of war is narrative disclosure, not line-of-sight math
Tiered: **hidden pins** first (staged locations and events simply don't exist on the players' map until the GM reveals them — and a reveal is itself a timeline event), **painted fog** behind it (the GM masks regions of the map image and unmasks them gesturally; each unmask lands in the timeline). Declined: automatic reveal-by-travel radius fog — it smuggles in position tracking and movement simulation, which belongs to VTTs. Fog in Hearsay is GM-authored disclosure of the story's world, full stop (2026-07-16).

## One shared world map per campaign; it may grow at the edges
Hearsay tracks the story at large and one world map sells that best (settled 2026-07-16). A map-per-scale hierarchy (region → city → dungeon) was declined as navigation design the concept doesn't need. Parked, not refused: as a campaign outgrows the drawn world, the GM replaces the map image with a larger one anchoring the old map inside it, pins carrying over, the growth itself a timeline event — expansion in the spirit of one-world-one-map, and the fork to reach for before hierarchy ever is.

## The grid is reference, not mechanics
An optional overlay — hex, square, or freeform (none), freeform the default so a napkin map stays first-class — set by the GM with cell size and offset (2026-07-16). It exists to give places names ("the ruins at J7"), optionally snap pins, and speak hexcrawl to tables that think in hexes. It measures nothing and resolves nothing; the not-a-VTT refusal holds at the grid line.

## Dropping a pin is a deliberate act, not a byproduct of touching the map
The map is the browse surface, so an ordinary tap means "look here," never "record here." The owner arms placement (a "＋ Pin" affordance) and the *next* tap places a single pin, after which the mode disarms; outside that mode the map only pans and zooms (settled 2026-07-16). Declined: incidental placement — the original prototype opened the event editor on any near-stationary tap, which put pin creation in permanent conflict with pan/zoom and let a mis-aimed touch mint canon. The deeper reason is the layer model: dropping a pin is canon-shaped authorship, and authorship should feel chosen. Same pass, same surface: pins counter-scale against the viewport transform so they hold a roughly constant, legible screen size at any zoom (they previously rode the scaled world and shrank to specks / ballooned). Parked, not refused: clustering + spiderfy for pins stacked at one coordinate across sessions — the real answer once a campaign accretes, deferred to the post-sync map-legibility pass. *(Note 2026-07-17: the pin/event split below dissolves the stacked-pins case this parked — repeat events accumulate at one pin. Clustering would only ever matter for genuinely distinct sites at near-identical coordinates, which is rarer and can wait for evidence.)*

## Players may propose pins; the owner authors canon from the proposal
The canon/testimony split extends to *placement*: a player who notices "something happened here" can suggest a pin, but the owner authors the canonical pin from it (settled 2026-07-16). Acceptance is adopt-and-edit — it opens the event editor pre-filled from the proposal so the owner confirms placement, type, and name before it becomes canon, keeping canon geography in one pair of hands. A pending proposal is visible only to its proposer and the owner (table-private; the shared map stays free of pre-canon speculation). Modeled as a separate `proposals` axis, never entangled with the `hidden`/fog dimension; on accept, the created event carries `proposedBy` provenance so the pin credits the player who noticed — the suggestion itself a small act of testimony. Authority-by-layer holds unbroken: the owner owns canon geography (accept, decline, final placement) and never edits the player's words. Declined: proposals-as-events with a `status` flag, which would force every fog/scrubber/warband consumer to special-case a state that has nothing to do with disclosure. *(Backend note 2026-07-17: the original Supabase RLS mapping is superseded by the mainline fork below — the target is Cloudflare Functions + storage. The authority shape carries unchanged: owner-only writes on pins, member-owns-their-own-row on proposals with owner-only status updates.)*

## The warband page is a living document; testimony is not
Each player owns a self-authored warband/character page — the answer to "who are you playing?", in their own voice (2026-07-16). Unlike testimony it is freely editable by its author, which is honest to what it describes (rosters change, soldiers die), with edits snapshotted under a session stamp so the scrubber shows the warband as it stood at any point. No stats, points math, or roster validation — that's rules content, refused. This layer also absorbed the non-spatial-events question: "on the road" scenes are testimony on the nearest meaningful pin or self-description on the warband page; no off-map margin in v1.

## Campaigns conclude by explicit GM act only
A concluded campaign becomes a read-only archive: closed to new events, scrubbable forever (settled 2026-07-16). No mortality clock exists — a deliberate inversion of Fragments, whose ephemerality ethos needs urgency; campaigns hiatus for months and come back, and a timer would punish exactly the low-energy month the family principles protect. Leaning (not yet settled): testimony slots stay fillable even after conclusion — late is fine, forever, and a slot filled a year later costs canon nothing.

## What Hearsay refuses
Not a VTT (no tokens, initiative, dice, or combat resolution — Hearsay owns the between-sessions layer, not table time). No rules content (system-agnostic by refusal, not feature matrix). No worldbuilding wiki (canon covers only what the campaign has touched; an entity with no event has no page — World Anvil and Kanka own the encyclopedia). No scheduling, chat, likes, or scoreboards (reading each other's testimony is the only social loop). No AI summarization of testimony (a generated recap is exactly the flattening the canon/testimony split exists to prevent). (2026-07-16)

## A pin is a place; events accumulate at it
Settled 2026-07-16. The first draft conflated pin and event (one pin per battle). Reframed: the **pin is a site**, dropped once, and events stack on it session over session — session 5's battle and session 9's battle land on the same ruins. The lineage of a site is the value ("what happened here first?"), and the anticipated stacked-pins clustering problem dissolves: revisited terrain is one pin deepening, not a pile needing UI. The scrubber gets richer for free — sites visibly thicken instead of pins raining down — and the grid's "the ruins at J7" was always naming a place; now the data model agrees. Site canon may accrete from history: the owner can leave a line the environment remembers (the rotting soldier, the scorched tower) that future events at the site inherit. Guard: site canon is only ever *what events left behind* — a site described with no event having happened is the World Anvil location page Hearsay refuses to be.

## Marks: testimony left in the world, unattributed at a glance
Settled 2026-07-16. A player may promote **one line of an event's testimony** into a **mark** — graffiti surfaced on the pin itself, the Dark Souls player-note brought to the campaign map. Deliberately not a new content type: the contribution ritual stays one journal entry, and the mark is a highlight pulled from it, so there is no second ask to survive the low-energy month. Earned by presence (you can only mark a site where you have testimony), immutable like all testimony, and *possibly false* — a lying mark is just subjective testimony, no new rules needed. Marks surface **unattributed at a glance** ("someone scrawled here: …"), authorship discoverable only by opening the testimony it came from — you hear the words before you know the mouth, the hearsay mechanic in miniature. A **hard brevity cap** (one sentence, ~100 characters) is the one place the app imposes a ceiling where everywhere else "one sentence" is a floor: brevity is what makes it graffiti rather than a plaque. No replies — a mark can't be answered, only outlasted — so the no-chat refusal holds. Marks inherit their event's session stamp and age in the scrubber. The owner's site-canon line and the players' marks are the same surface seen from two layers: the environment remembering officially and unofficially, side by side. *(Model note 2026-07-17: in the V1 schema a mark is a nullable `markText` on the testimony row rather than its own table — the fork's own words, "not a new content type, a highlight pulled from it," finally reflected in the data shape. Nothing about the behavior changes.)*

## Testimony closes on the table's clock: editable until the next session's first event
Settled 2026-07-16. Immutable-after-save needs a grace window (the ten-seconds-later typo), but a timer-based window isn't testimony and punishes hiatus. The window is the campaign's own clock: a testimony entry stays freely editable by its author until the **next session's first event lands** on the campaign, then it closes forever. Sessions-are-the-clock doing the work — no session, no closure, no rot; a table on a three-month hiatus loses nothing. Declined: fixed-duration edit windows (calendar time is not the campaign's time) and edit-forever-with-history (a revisable account isn't testimony; the warband page is the layer that gets to live).

## The first build is a portable, local-first PWA; sync is deferred, not designed-around
The prototype is a buildless Progressive Web App (static files, vanilla ES modules, IndexedDB) rather than a spreadsheet, a native app, or a server-backed web app (2026-07-16). Rationale: the table is ready to use something now, and a portable PWA is the roadmap that can *evolve beyond* — installable on phones, offline, no accounts — without committing to infrastructure before the validation question is answered. Local-first with `.hearsay.json` export/import is the honest stand-in for multiplayer: it lets the closed table pass a campaign device-to-device today, and names sync (a real backend) as the explicit next rung rather than pretending it isn't needed. Declined for this stage: a backend/auth (premature before players are proven to write), and painted fog / map-growth / archive-shelf polish (all in the vision, none load-bearing for the test). The refusals hold unchanged — not a VTT, no rules content, no wiki, no AI summarization. **Superseded 2026-07-17:** the owner's V1 requirements — join-by-code membership, owner-ratified invites, and sessions that update fast on every device — are exactly the new information this entry reserved judgment for. A local-first record cannot serve a multi-device table, and testimony trapped in one phone's IndexedDB dies with the phone. See "The mainline is the Vite prototype" and "V1 is server-authoritative" below; the spike is archived with its learnings harvested, not lost.

## Hearsay is a Litany-family sibling: on the storefront, never inside the apps
Settled 2026-07-17, closing the family-relationship fork from both sides (mirrored in Litany's decisions.md). Hearsay stays its own product in its own repo — the 2026-07-16 move out of Fragments' docs extends to Litany: shared mission and conventions, no shared code or product decisions. It will never ship as a feature of a Litany game app: those apps refuse persistent state, history, identity, and user-generated content by pinned principle (Litany's Principle 11 and Conclave forks), and their static offline-PWA architecture can't carry Hearsay's storage or invite identity — a merge would reuse nothing but the brand while putting testimony inside Litany's publisher-facing reference surface. The one incorporation surface is Litany's landing: when Hearsay can run a real session night, it earns a workshop plate and Ledger line there in its honest preview state, per Litany's graduation-ladder convention. Roads not taken: a campaign tab inside the Frostgrave app (blocked outright), an immediate cross-link from the game apps (deferred until the live-campaign validation answers its open sub-questions), and the domain question (`hearsay.litany.app` vs its own) — deliberately held open until graduation, when the shape of real testimony content will say whether it belongs under litany.app at all.

## The mainline is the Vite prototype; the local-first spike retires to the archive
Settled 2026-07-17. Two prototypes were built in parallel by sessions that could not see each other: a buildless local-first PWA in `app/` (IndexedDB, `.hearsay.json` export/import, GitHub Pages workflow) and the Vite + TypeScript + Vitest line in `src/` (the agreed prototype-scope stack carried from Fragments, the pin/event-split data model, a store that enforces the settled forks in code). The Vite line is the mainline: it embodies the newer settled model — pin/event split, marks, the grace window, all of which postdate the spike — and the owner's V1 requirements need the backend the local-first thesis deferred. The spike retires to `docs/archive/local-first-pwa/` (its GitHub Pages workflow removed with it), and its real contributions are harvested rather than lost: the deliberate-pin-placement fork, the proposal pattern, the `--pin-k` constant-size pin rendering, the competitive-scan findings (testimony juxtaposition as the lead post-V1 candidate; no surveyed tool centers per-seat testimony), and the headless-driver verification practice. Road not taken: merging the two codebases — they share no files, but two runtimes of one product is a fork pretending to be a family, and every future feature would be built twice.

## Membership follows the proposal pattern: players share the code, the owner seats the table
Settled 2026-07-17, the owner's call. One rotatable **join code** per campaign. Anyone arriving through it becomes a **pending** member who can write immediately — their testimony visible only to themselves and the owner — and one owner tap flips them **active**, making their words part of the shared record. Owner-added players are born active. Players may share the code freely: bringing someone to the door is a player act, seating them at the table is a canon act — the proposal pattern (players propose, the owner ratifies) extended from geography to membership. Declined: a separate invites table (the code plus a member status column carries everything); per-invite codes (ceremony a closed table doesn't need); and approval-gates-*writing* (a pending player who can't write loses their first session's testimony to bureaucracy — gating visibility instead costs nothing and honors late-is-fine-forever).

## V1 is server-authoritative, and ships without a service worker
Settled 2026-07-17, driven by the owner's observed iOS behavior (an installed home-screen app pinning a stale cache until the icon is deleted and re-added) and Litany's apex-cutover lesson (a service worker outlives the app that registered it and must be actively evicted). Two rules. First, **the server is the source of truth for campaign data**: devices fetch fresh (network-first), refetch on focus, and short-poll while a pin surface is open, so a session night sees new events quickly on every phone; device storage is at most a cache, never the record — testimony is too precious to live in one phone's IndexedDB. Second, **V1 ships no service worker at all**: a plain web app with hashed assets and an always-fresh index.html cannot pin itself stale. Installability is deferred, not refused — it returns only as a deliberately built, update-disciplined worker (versioned, skipWaiting/claim on update, kill-switch-ready), never as a checkbox in a build plugin. Declined: websockets/real-time sync — VTT-grade ceremony a between-sessions tool doesn't need; polling is honest for a table-sized group.

## The first validation table is a Frostgrave campaign, chosen as the hard test
The unproven behavior is player-side: will players write testimony unnagged? (GM record-keeping is proven by every campaign wiki ever.) Frostgrave stresses every distinctive part at once — competitive, often GM-less, rosters that change every game, rival testimony about the same battle from both sides (2026-07-16). Cheaply testable before any build: three or four sessions on a shared image with numbered pins and a doc per player. If players write, plural memory has legs; if only the organizer writes, the concept collapses into a worldbuilding wiki it refuses to be.

**Closed as validated 2026-07-16, without running the paper test.** The table's local Discord already produces the behavior in the wild — players posting their own accounts of battles, unnagged — it just lacks tooling that survives the tabletop. The hypothesis was "will players write?"; they demonstrably do, so the paper test would only re-measure what the Discord already shows. Build proceeds. Sub-questions the paper test was meant to answer from behavior (competitive sealing, marks uptake) move to the live campaign on the built tool instead.
